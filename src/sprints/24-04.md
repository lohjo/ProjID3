## Sources for Deriving the Full 1-D PDE System

Here is the minimal, sequenced source list — use it tonight in this exact order.

### Step 1 — The control-volume mass balance (30 min)

**Bird, Stewart & Lightfoot — *Transport Phenomena*, Ch. 11 (The Equations of Change for Multicomponent Systems)**

This is where you get the general species continuity equation from first principles. You already understand the divergence theorem from your reading, so the step from the integral form to the differential form will click immediately. The packed-bed version is just a 1-D volume-averaged reduction of this.

If you don't have BSL access tonight, the derivation in **Chapter 3 of the CO₂_Sorbent_Study_Plan.docx** (already in your project files, §3.2.1–3.2.3) gives you every equation written out with every term labelled. Treat that as your template and *re-derive each line yourself* on paper — don't just copy it.

### Step 2 — The Linear Driving Force (LDF) kinetic model (20 min)

**Ruthven — *Principles of Adsorption and Adsorption Processes*, Ch. 6 or 8**

The LDF solid-phase equation `(1−ε)ρ_p ∂q/∂t = k_a a_p (1−ε)(C − C*)` comes from approximating intraparticle diffusion as a single lumped resistance. Ruthven gives the derivation and the Glueckauf relation `k = 15 D_mic / r_p²`. If you don't have Ruthven, the study plan §3.2.2 derives it and explains the physical meaning of every symbol.

### Step 3 — The energy balance with heat-of-adsorption coupling (30 min)

The energy balance is the hardest equation because it has two coupled sub-equations (gas temperature T_g and solid temperature T_s). The cleanest derivation path is:

1. Write a control-volume energy balance for the gas phase: convection + axial conduction + interphase heat transfer + wall heating.
2. Write a control-volume energy balance for the solid phase: interphase heat transfer + heat released/consumed by adsorption/desorption.
3. The coupling term is `(−ΔH_ads) ρ_p (1−ε) ∂q/∂t` — the heat source on the solid side equals the heat released when molecules adsorb (or absorbed when they desorb during regeneration).

Again, §3.2.3 of the study plan has the full equations. Your job is to justify every term from first principles on paper before showing it to Prof. Erik.

### Step 4 — The Langmuir isotherm closure (15 min)

The isotherm closes the system: it relates C* (the equilibrium gas-phase concentration) to q (the solid loading) and T. The temperature-dependent form you need is:

```
q* = q_m(T) · b(T) · C / (1 + b(T) · C)
b(T) = b_0 · exp(ΔH_ads / RT)
q_m(T) = q_m0 · exp(χ/T)
```

The statistical mechanics derivation is in §4.3 of the study plan (grand canonical partition function → Langmuir). For tonight, you only need the result — the derivation is enrichment for next week's math track.

### Step 5 — Check the system is closed and count equations vs unknowns (10 min)

Once you have all four PDEs + the isotherm, verify:
- **Unknowns:** C(z,t), q(z,t), T_g(z,t), T_s(z,t) — four fields.
- **Equations:** gas mass balance, solid mass balance (LDF), gas energy balance, solid energy balance — four PDEs, closed by the Langmuir relation giving C* = f(q, T_s).

Write down all boundary conditions: at z = 0, specify the inlet purge gas concentration (C = 0 for a clean purge), inlet gas temperature (T_g = T_regen). At z = L, a Neumann (zero-gradient) outflow condition. Initial conditions: q = q_sat (fully loaded), T_g = T_s = T_ambient.

### Quick reference — the four equations in compact form

For easy checking against your derivation:

```
Gas mass:   ε ∂C/∂t = −u ∂C/∂z + D_ax ∂²C/∂z² − k_a a_p (1−ε)(C − C*)

Solid mass: (1−ε)ρ_p ∂q/∂t = k_a a_p (1−ε)(C − C*)

Gas energy: ε ρ_g c_pg ∂T_g/∂t = −ρ_g c_pg u ∂T_g/∂z 
                                  + λ_ax ∂²T_g/∂z² 
                                  + h_f a_p (T_s − T_g) 
                                  + Q_wall(z,t)

Solid energy: (1−ε)ρ_p c_ps ∂T_s/∂t = h_f a_p (T_g − T_s) 
                                       + (−ΔH_ads) ρ_p (1−ε) ∂q/∂t
```

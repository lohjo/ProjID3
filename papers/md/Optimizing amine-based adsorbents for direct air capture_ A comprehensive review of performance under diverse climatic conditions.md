A B S T R A C T


Since the Industrial Revolution, the rapid rise in greenhouse gas emissions has driven a concerning increase in
atmospheric CO2 levels, resulting in global warming and climate-related crises. In response, direct air capture
(DAC) technology has emerged as a crucial negative-emissions solution, uniquely positioned to directly remove
CO2 from the atmosphere, beyond merely targeting industrial emissions. This review provides a comprehensive
analysis of solid amine-based adsorbents for DAC, focusing on both performance optimization and economic
viability—two essential factors for successful commercialization. A systematic classification of solid amine-based
adsorbents is presented based on sorbent classes, amine types, and support materials including metal-organic
frameworks (MOFs), silica, alumina, and covalent organic frameworks (COFs). Special emphasis is given to
the performance of these adsorbents under varying environmental conditions, exploring humidity levels in the
range of 0–100 % relative humidity and temperatures from − 20 to 40 [◦] C. By examining these factors, the review
establishes regional optimization strategies to enhance the practical deployment of DAC technology worldwide.
The insights provide key recommendations for optimizing DAC deployment in diverse climatic contexts and
identify priority areas for further research and development to drive the technology forward.

**1.** **Introduction**


The escalating climate crisis, primarily driven by anthropogenic
greenhouse gas emissions, is one of the most urgent challenges of the
21st century [1]. Central to this crisis is the sharp rise in atmospheric
carbon dioxide (CO2) concentration since the Industrial Revolution. As
reported by the CarbonTracker, atmospheric CO2 levels reached a record
up to 431 parts per million (ppm) in the United States in 2018, marking
almost 50 % increase from the pre-industrial level of ~280 ppm [2,3].
This alarming rise is particularly concerning, as CO2 is a potent green­
house gas with a long atmospheric lifetime, playing a significant role in
global warming and wide-ranging impacts on climate patterns, ecosys­
tems, and human societies [4,5].

The urgency of the situation is further highlighted by the immense
scale of annual CO2 emissions. In 2021, global CO2 emissions from fossil
fuels and industry rebounded to 36.3 billion metric tons, nearly reaching
the peak levels of 2019 following a temporary decline during the COVID19 pandemic [6]. Moreover, the Global Carbon Budget report presented
at the U.N. COP29 climate summit in Azerbaijan projects global CO2
emissions to reach 41.6 billion metric tons in 2024, an increase from
40.6 billion metric tons in 2023 [7,8]. This upward trend starkly con­
trasts with the critical need to drastically cut emissions to avert the worst
effects of climate change. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate
Change (IPCC) has stressed that limiting global warming to 1.5 [◦] C above
pre-industrial levels–a threshold beyond which climate impacts are
projected to become significantly more severe – requires reducing global
net CO2 emissions by 45 % from 2010 levels by 2030 and achieving net
zero emissions by around 2050 [9].

However, even with aggressive emissions reduction strategies, many
climate models indicate that achieving climate stabilization targets will
require not only drastic cuts in emissions but also active removal of CO2
from the atmosphere [10]. Current projections suggest that by 2050, it
may be necessary to remove 10–20 billion tons of CO2 annually to meet
climate goals [11,12]. This has led to growing interest in negative
emissions technologies (NETs), which aim to extract CO2 directly from
the air. Among these technologies, direct air capture (DAC) has gained
particular attention from researchers, policymakers, and industry
stakeholders due to its promising potential.



DAC technology offers several unique advantages that make it a
critical component of the climate mitigation strategy [13–15]. Unlike
point-source carbon capture systems, which are typically limited to large
stationary emitters like power plants and industrial facilities, DAC can
be deployed flexibly, addressing emissions from distributed and mobile
sources that are difficult to decarbonize by other means [16,17].
Furthermore, DAC offers the potential to remove historical emissions
already present in the atmosphere, creating a pathway not only to sta­
bilize but also to potentially reverse some of the CO2 accumulation from
past centuries [18]. Another significant advantage of DAC is its rela­
tively small land footprint compared to other negative emissions ap­
proaches, such as afforestation or bioenergy with carbon capture and
storage (BECCS) [19–21]. This makes DAC particularly attractive in
regions with limited land or competing demands for land uses. Addi­
tionally, DAC systems can be co-located with renewable energy sources
or geological storage sites, optimizing both efficiency and environ­
mental impact of the carbon removal processes [22,23].

Despite these promising attributes, the widespread deployment of
DAC faces substantial challenges, such as high energy requirements and
high capital costs. The core difficulty arises from the extremely low CO2
concentration in the atmosphere—around 0.04 %, which necessitates
processing vast volumes of air to capture considerable amounts of CO2

[13]. DAC requires between 5 and 15 GJ of energy per ton of CO2
captured [24]. This inherently energy-intensive process demands a
careful evaluation of the overall carbon balance and economic feasibility
of DAC systems.

A key factor in overcoming these challenges and advancing DAC
technology is the development and optimization of materials capable of
efficiently capturing CO2 under atmospheric conditions [25]. The per­
formance of these materials—typically solid sorbents or liquid sol­
vents—is crucial in determining the overall efficiency, energy
consumption, and cost-effectiveness of DAC systems [26]. However, the
effectiveness of these materials can vary significantly depending on the
specific environmental conditions. This variability in material perfor­
mance across different operating conditions represents a critical aspect
of DAC technology that has not been comprehensively explored in pre­
vious reviews [27]. The CO2 adsorption mechanisms and capacities of
capture materials are highly sensitive to factors such as temperature and
humidity, which can fluctuate considerably depending on geographical
location and diurnal/seasonal variations [28]. For instance, the
adsorption capacity of many materials decreases at higher temperatures
due to the exothermic nature of the adsorption process, while humidity
can either enhance or inhibit CO2 capture depending on the specific
material and mechanism involved [2,29].

Understanding these performance variations is essential for several
reasons. First, it enables more accurate assessment and comparison of
different materials under realistic operating conditions, offering valu­
able insights for material selection and system design across diverse
geographical contexts. Second, it underscores the importance of devel­
oping materials that can sustain high performance across a wide range of
environmental conditions, thereby enhancing the versatility and scal­
ability of DAC technology. Lastly, this understanding can direct research
efforts toward tailoring materials for specific climatic zones or creating
adaptive systems capable of optimizing performance under varying
conditions.


**2.** **Conventional materials for CO2** **capture and their limitations**
**in DAC**


_2.1._ _Aqueous amine solvents_


Amine solvents have long been the backbone of CO2 capture from
flue gas streams, valued for their high selectivity and high absorption
capacity [30,31]. The most commonly used amines include mono­
ethanolamine (MEA), methyldiethanolamine (MDEA), and diethanol­
amine (DEA), where CO2 reacts with amine groups to form carbamates

_Y. Jin et al.                                                                                                                    Renewable_ _and_ _Sustainable_ _Energy_ _Reviews_ _217_ _(2025)_ _115782_



or bicarbonates [32,33]. Among them, MEA is widely utilized in in­
dustrial applications due to its high reactivity with CO2 and relatively
low cost, achieving CO2 removal efficiencies of 85–90 % for flue gas
streams with CO2 concentrations ranging from 3 to 15 %. While MDEA
and DEA offer certain advantages over MEA, such as lower regeneration
energy requirements and reduced corrosiveness, they typically exhibit
slower reaction kinetics [32].

However, applying these amine solutions to DAC poses significant
challenges. The extremely low concentration of CO2 in ambient air
(approximately 340 ppm or 0.034 %) results in dramatically reduced
absorption rates and capacities in traditional amine solutions [34,35];
moreover, regenerating amine solutions requires substantial energy due
to the high specific heat of water, with MEA typically needing regen­
eration temperatures of 100–120 [◦] C and consuming 3.2–4.2 GJ tCO−2 1

[34]. The corrosive nature of amine solutions, especially at elevated
regeneration temperatures, also causes equipment deterioration and
generates volatile degradation products that can be environmentally
harmful [36]. In open-air DAC systems, water evaporation from amine
solutions becomes a considerable issue, increasing operational costs and
altering solution properties over time. These limitations have driven
researchers to investigate water-free solid adsorbents as potential al­
ternatives for DAC, aiming to overcome the inherent drawbacks of liquid
amine systems while maintaining high CO2 capture efficiency.


_2.2._ _Physisorption-based materials_


Physisorption-based DAC relies on weak, non-covalent inter­
actions—primarily van der Waals forces, electrostatic attractions, and
ion–quadrupole interactions—to bind CO2 molecules to the surface of
porous solids [37]. Unlike chemisorption, these interactions do not
involve the formation of chemical bonds, resulting in reversible
adsorption processes that typically require lower regeneration energy.
As a result, physisorption-based systems are suited for regeneration via
low-energy techniques, such as temperature swing adsorption (TSA) or
pressure swing adsorption (PSA), due to the weak and reversible nature
of CO2-sorbent interactions [38].

However, the inherently weak binding forces in physisorption result
in lower CO2 affinity at ambient concentrations (~400 ppm), limiting
their overall uptake capacity. The absence of strong chemical in­
teractions also results in slower kinetics and reduced equilibrium ca­
pacities unless the sorbent materials are highly optimized [38].
Therefore, to achieve effective CO2 capture, physisorbents must possess
a combination of high surface area, tailored pore size distribution, and
superior thermal and mechanical stability [39,40]. Common phys­
isorbents include zeolites, activated carbons, and MOFs. Each category
offers unique pore structures, surface chemistries, and adsorption be­
haviors. However, their performance can be significantly affected by
environmental conditions, such as humidity and temperature. For
instance, zeolites are highly efficient under dry conditions but exhibit
reduced performance in the presence of moisture. Activated carbons, on
the other hand, offer robustness but generally require optimization of
pore structures to enhance adsorption efficiency.


_2.2.1._ _Zeolites_

Zeolites, crystalline aluminosilicates with well-defined pore struc­
tures and high surface areas, have been extensively studied for CO2
capture due to their molecular sieving properties and tunable pore sizes

[41,42]. Common zeolites such as 13X and 5A have shown promising
CO2 adsorption capacities, reaching up to 5.5 mmol g [−] [1 ] under ambient
conditions [43,44]. However, their application in DAC faces several
hurdles. One major issue is their high sensitivity to moisture, as water
vapor competes strongly with CO2 for adsorption sites, substantially
reducing CO2 capture capacity under the humid conditions typically
found in ambient air [45]. Additionally, at the low CO2 partial pressures
encountered in DAC, the CO2/N2 selectivity of zeolites decreases sub­
stantially, which limits their overall effectiveness [35]. Furthermore, the



relatively strong physisorption of CO2 on zeolites necessitates significant
energy for desorption, typically in the range of 2.5–3.5 GJ tCO−2 1, which
adversely affects the energy efficiency of the capture process [46].
Despite these limitations, ongoing research continues to explore modi­
fied zeolites and novel synthesis techniques to enhance their perfor­
mance towards DAC applications.


_2.2.2._ _Activated carbons_

Activated carbons (ACs) present an attractive option for CO2 capture
due to their low cost, high thermal stability, and ease of regeneration

[47,48]. These highly porous materials, featured with large surface
areas and tunable pore size distributions, can achieve CO2 adsorption
capacities of 2–3 mmol g [−] [1 ] under ambient conditions [49]. A key
advantage of ACs in DAC is their hydrophobicity, which makes them less
susceptible to moisture interference than zeolites [50]. Furthermore, the
weak interactions between CO2 and AC surfaces facilitate easier regen­
eration, typically requiring 1.5–2.5 GJ tCO−2 1, which is lower than the
energy needed for zeolites [51]. However, ACs face limitations in DAC
applications. The non-specific nature of physisorption on ACs results in
poor CO2/N2 selectivity, especially at the low CO2 partial pressure
characteristic of ambient air [52]. Additionally, ACs generally exhibit
lower CO2 adsorption capacities than zeolites under similar conditions,
which may necessitate larger adsorbent volumes to achieve equivalent
CO2 capture. Despite these challenges, ACs remain a topic of interest in
DAC research due to their low cost and ease of production, with ongoing
efforts focused on surface modifications and pore structure optimization
to enhance performance [53].



_2.2.3._ _Metal-organic frameworks_

Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs) represent a new class of porous
materials that have garnered significant attention in the field of CO2
capture. Composed of metal ions or clusters coordinated to organic li­
gands, MOFs offer unprecedented structural and chemical tunability,
enabling the design of materials with properties optimized for CO2
capture [54,55]. Certain MOFs have demonstrated exceptionally high
CO2 adsorption capacities, exceeding 8 mmol g [−] [1 ] under ambient con­
ditions, outperforming many conventional adsorbents [56,57]. With
surface areas that can surpass 7000 m [2 ] g [−] [1], MOFs provide numerous
adsorption sites for CO2 [58]; moreover, their tunable pore sizes and
chemical functionalities may enhance CO2 selectivity and capacity,
making them well-suited for capturing CO2 at the low concentrations
encountered in DAC [35]. Some MOFs also exhibit low heats of
adsorption for CO2, potentially reducing regeneration energy re­
quirements compared to other physisorbents [59].

Despite these advantages, MOFs still face significant challenges in
DAC. Many MOFs are susceptible to degradation or performance
reduction in the presence of water vapor, which is a critical issue
considering the humidity in ambient air. The synthesis of MOFs often
requires expensive precursors and complex procedures, limiting their
large-scale application and economic viability [60]. Additionally, some
MOFs lack the mechanical and thermal stability required for long-term
cycling in industrial conditions [61]. Despite these hurdles, the highly
tunable nature of MOFs continues to drive research, with current efforts
focusing on developing water-stable MOFs and cost-effective synthesis
methods to unlock their potential for DAC applications.


_2.3._ _Chemisorption-based materials_


The limitations of physisorption-based materials under the low CO2
partial pressure conditions of DAC have driven researchers to explore
chemisorption-based approaches. Chemisorption, involving the forma­
tion of chemical bonds between CO2 and the sorbent, offers several
advantages for DAC applications. Due to the stronger interactions
involved, these materials can achieve significantly higher CO2 capacities
than physisorbents under DAC-relevant conditions. Additionally, the
chemical reaction-driven process of chemisorption typically proceeds

more rapidly than physisorption, offering faster kinetics-a crucial factor
for processing large volumes of air. The selective formation of chemical
bonds with CO2 also enhances specificity over other atmospheric com­
ponents, addressing a key challenge of DAC.

To leverage these advantages while addressing the limitations of
liquid amine solutions, researchers have focused on developing solid
sorbents impregnated with amine-containing compounds [62,63]. These
solid sorbents aim to combine the high CO2 affinity of amines with the
practical benefits of solid supports, such as reduced corrosion and easier
handling. Two main approaches have been explored:
amine-impregnated materials, where porous supports like silica or ACs
are loaded with liquid amines such as polyethyleneimine (PEI) or tet­
raethylenepentamine (TEPA), and amine-grafted materials, where
amine groups are covalently bonded to the surface of porous supports to
enhance stability and minimize amine leaching [64,65].

Amine-functionalized solid sorbents have demonstrated promising
DAC performance, with CO2 capacities exceeding 3 mmol g [−] [1 ] under
ambient conditions and exhibiting good cyclic stability [66]. Never­
theless, challenges remain in optimizing regeneration energy re­
quirements, preventing amine degradation and scaling up production.
The development of these materials marks a significant advance in DAC
technology, combining the advantages of chemisorption with the prac­
tical benefits of solid supports. Ongoing research focuses on further
enhancing the performance, stability, and cost-effectiveness of these
sorbents to enable large-scale deployment of DAC technologies.



**3.** **Amine-impregnated solid sorbents for DAC**


_3.1._ _Classification of amine-supported solid sorbents_


Amine-impregnated solid sorbents have emerged as one of the most
promising candidates for DAC applications (Fig. 1). These hybrid ma­
terials effectively combine the high CO2 affinity and selectivity of
amines with the structural advantages and stability of solid supports,
offering a practical solution to the limitations associated with traditional
liquid amine systems and pure solid adsorbents [34,35]. By integrating
the strengths of both chemisorption and solid material frameworks,
amine-impregnated sorbents offer enhanced CO2 capture performance,
improved operational stability, and more ease of handling.

Amine-supported solid materials are typically categorized into three
distinct classes based on the method of incorporating amine molecules
into the solid support structure [67]. Each class presents specific ad­
vantages and poses unique challenges:

**Class 1 materials** are created by physically impregnating amine
molecules into the pores of support materials. These materials can
achieve high amine loading density and exhibit good thermal stability.
However, the primary drawback lies in amine leaching during extended
cycling, due to the relatively weak physical interactions between the
amine molecules and the support [68].

**Class 2 materials** involve the covalent bonding of amine groups to
the support surface using various grafting techniques. This chemical

_3.3._ _Optimization challenges and solutions_


_3.3.1._ _Selection of appropriate amine candidates_

The selection of appropriate amine types for DAC applications re­
quires a careful balance between key performance factors, such as ki­
netics, capacity, and stability. While primary amines typically exhibit
faster reaction kinetics and higher initial CO2 uptake, they are prone to
forming urea species through the reaction of carbamic acid in­
termediates with free amines. This reaction leads to permanent amine
deactivation, decreasing CO2 capture capacity over time [73]. The
deactivation mechanism can be described as follows:

R-NHCOOH + R-NH2 → R-NH-CO-NH-R + H2O               (5)

To address this issue, several strategies have been developed.


A. **Incorporating crosslinking agents:** This strategy involves con­

verting primary amines to secondary or tertiary amines, thereby
reducing the likelihood of urea formation [74].



_3.3.2._ _Sorbent morphology on DAC efficiency_

The morphology and structural properties of solid sorbents are crit­
ical parameters influencing their performance in DAC systems. Key
physical characteristics—such as surface area, pore volume, pore
interconnectivity, and mechanical stability—affect not only amine
dispersion and CO2 adsorption kinetics but also the long-term cyclability
and robustness under cyclic operation. Porous supports with large
mesopore and macropore volumes facilitate higher amine loading and
improved diffusion properties, enabling faster adsorption–desorption
dynamics [77]. However, excessive amine loading can cause pore
blockage, hindering diffusion and reducing overall efficiency. This
highlights the need for an optimal balance between pore morphology
and functional group accessibility [25,78].

To address limitations such as pressure drop and poor heat/mass
transfer in packed beds [79,80], structured sorbents, particularly hon­
eycomb monoliths, have been proposed as effective alternatives [81,82].
These monoliths offer advantages including low pressure drop,
enhanced heat and mass transfer characteristics, and scalability.
Commonly constructed from materials like cordierite, they are ther­
mally stable, mechanically durable, and can be coated with
high-surface-area oxides (e.g., γ-Al2O3) before amine functionalization

[83]. For instance, Sakwa-Novak et al. demonstrated that
PEI-functionalized alumina monoliths exhibited comparable or even
superior CO2 adsorption capacities compared to powdered counterparts.
Furthermore, they demonstrated significantly better stability under
humid steam cycling, likely due to improved amine retention within
macroporous channels [84].

The impact of relative humidity (RH) is another crucial factor
affecting DAC performance. Water vapor can enhance CO2 adsorption by
facilitating the formation of bicarbonate species. However, sorbent
morphology strongly influences water retention and stability under
humid conditions [29,85]. For instance, monolithic PEI-impregnated
sorbents have demonstrated improved stability under humid steam



B. **Utilizing amine blends:** Blending different amine types can opti­

C. **Developing sterically hindered amines:** These specially designed

In practice, the optimal solution often involves a carefully designed
mixture of different amine types. This approach balances the faster ki­

tertiary amines. As a result, it enables the development of sorbent ma­
terials that can maintain high CO2 capture capacity while minimizing
degradation over multiple capture-regeneration cycles.

depending on the interaction between sorbent morphology and the ad­
sorbent’s chemical properties. Structured materials designed to accom­
modate both CO2 and moisture, without significant degradation of the
active amine site, are crucial for optimizing performance under realistic
DAC conditions. Overall, the integration of optimized porosity, mono­
lithic structuring, and humidity-tolerant materials offers a robust
pathway for enhancing the efficiency, stability, and scalability of DAC
technologies. Improved morphological control can contribute to better
process intensification, energy efficiency, and mechanical durability, all
of which are essential for successful real-world deployment.

_4.1._ _Ethylenediamine-functionalized MOFs_


Building on the fundamental characteristics of MOFs discussed
earlier, amine functionalization has emerged as a promising strategy to
enhance their CO2 capture performance under DAC conditions. The
presence of open metal sites (OMS) in MOF structures—specific coor­
dination sites where metal ions are not fully coordinated by organic
ligands—provides unique opportunities for amine functionalization [86,
87]. These OMS can serve as anchoring points for amine groups,
enhancing CO2 capture through both physisorption and chemisorption
mechanisms.

Among various amine-functionalized MOFs, ethylenediaminemodified Mg2(dobpdc) (EN-Mg2(dobpdc)) has demonstrated particu­

one of the highest reported values for MOF-based materials under these
conditions [90]. Furthermore, EN-Mg2(dopdc) maintains robust per­
formance during multiple adsorption-desorption cycles, with only a 6 %
loss in capacity after five cycles using regeneration at 150 [◦] C for 2 h.

While EN-Mg2(dobpdc) demonstrates impressive performance met­

challenges must be addressed for practical implementation in DAC. The
multi-step synthesis of EN-Mg2(dobpdc) involves intricate procedures

regeneration temperature of 150 [◦] C poses a significant energy demand,
impacting the overall energy efficiency and operating costs of the DAC
process. Furthermore, the complexity of the synthesis process presents
challenges for industrial-scale production.

_4.2._ _Covalent organic frameworks (COFs)_


Covalent organic frameworks (COFs) represent an emerging class of
porous crystalline materials constructed through strong covalent bonds
such as C-C, C-N, C-O, B-O, C=N, and C-Si [91,92]. They are synthesized
via reversible condensation reactions between two molecular species,
and the reversibility of these reactions is enabled by hydrolysis
back-reactions. Among various support materials for DAC applications,
COFs have garnered significant attention due to their high permanent
surface areas, surpassing those of hybrid MOFs or inorganic zeolites

[91].

The performance data summarized in Table 1 illustrates the current
advancements in COF development for DAC applications, highlighting
the trade-off between stability, selectivity, and capture capacity. The
first discovered boron-based COFs, synthesized via condensation re­
actions forming B-O bonds, exhibited promising characteristics
including high crystallinity, low densities, and large surface areas
(750–3620 m [2 ] g [−] [1] ) [93,94]. For instance, COF-102 demonstrated a high
CO uptake capacity of 27.3 mmol g [−] [1 ] at 55 bar and 298 K, while COF-10
showed 23 mmol g [−] [1 ] under the same conditions, due to the higher BET
surface area of COF-102 than COF-10 (3620 _versus_ 1760 m [2 ] g [−] [1] ).
However, boron-based COFs face significant limitations in practical DAC
application due to their instability in moist air or water, which is
attributed to the hydrolysis of B-O bonds through reversible condensa­
tion reactions [95]. Although beneficial for creating crystalline struc­
tures, this makes boron-based COFs particularly vulnerable to moisture
exposure.

In contrast, triazine-based (synthesized via cyclotrimerization) and
imine-based COFs (formed through Schiff base condensation) demon­
strate superior stability in water and most organic solvents. Among
triazine-based COFs, Fluorinated Covalent Triazine Framework (FCTF1) shows exceptional CO2 capture performance with CO2 uptake of 4.7
mmol g [−] [1 ] at 1 bar and 273 K [96]. The incorporation of fluorine groups
in FCTF-1 serves dual purposes: The strongly polar C-F bonds enhance
CO2 capture through electrostatic interactions, while simultaneously
providing hydrophobic nature that maintains performance even in the
presence of moisture. Similarly, imine-based TpPa-1 shows 3.5 mmol
g [−] [1 ] under similar conditions [97]. The enhanced stability stems from
their fundamental chemical structure: Triazine-based COFs feature
strong C-N bonds in their triazine rings, while imine-based COFs can be
further stabilized through irreversible proton tautomerism from
enol-imine to keto-enamine form and intramolecular hydrogen bonding

performance capabilities, demonstrating potential for long-term prac­

The structure of COF-999 incorporates several features contributing
to its high efficiency. The use of hydrophobic building units (aromatic

_4.4._ _Amine-functionalized alumina adsorbents_


While silica has commonly serves as the primary support for solid
amine adsorbents, alumina-based supports have emerged as promising
alternatives that can overcome several limitations inherent to silicabased materials [108]. A key advantage of alumina (Al2O3) supports
lies in their superior structural stability, particularly under humid con­
ditions where silica-based materials often experience degradation,
leading to certain loss of CO2 capture capacity [109]. The alumina
supports partially form boehmite, an aluminum oxyhydroxide, upon
exposure to steam. The formation of boehmite crystals does not signif­
icantly affect CO2 adsorption [109]. Therefore, the alumina support
exhibits robust stability to structural changes during steam regeneration
due to the crystalline nature of boehmite.

PEI-impregnated γ- Al2O3 (30 wt%) retains _>_ 90 % of its initial CO2
adsorption capacity (1.7 mmol g [−] [1] ) under simulated air conditions for
up to 12 h of steam exposure (Table 4), but degrades to 0.66 mmol g [−] [1 ]

after 24 h due to PEI leaching [109]. Additionally, the γ-alumina support
undergoes partial hydration to form boehmite crystal phase at steam
exposure times of 90 min and longer, with this phase transition occur­
ring predominantly between 90 min and 12 h of treatment under
continuous steam flow at ~115 [◦] C. Notably, the presence of boehmite on
the sorbent surface does not significantly impact the amine efficiency of
impregnated PEI, indicating potential viability for CO2 capture from
ambient air with steam regeneration. Furthermore, PEI leaching, rather
than support structural changes, emerges as the primary factor affecting
long-term stability. Moreover, N2 physisorption data reveals that sam­
ples exposed to steam for 90 min and 12 h exhibit changes attributed to
PEI rearrangement within the support pores during steam cycling, rather
than boehmite formation.

Building upon understanding of alumina’s structural stability under
steam conditions, further optimization of alumina-based CO2 sorbents
can be achieved through acidity control. Pseudoboehmite support with
enhanced Brønsted acidic sites demonstrates superior PEI dispersion and
stability compared to the Lewis acidic sites in γ-Al2O3, achieving
exceptional CO2 uptake capacity of 4.9 mmol g [−] [1 ] with 25 % PEI loading

[110]. The strong interaction between PEI and -OH groups in support’s
enables 100 stable adsorption-desorption cycles with complete regen­
eration at just 150 [◦] C. This strategic tuning of support acidity, coupled
with the inherent structural stability of alumina to steam exposure,
presents a comprehensive approach to developing more efficient and
durable CO2 capture systems.

The regeneration of PEI-impregnated alumina sorbents can be ach­
ieved through either steam stripping at ~115 [◦] C or temperature swing at
150 [◦] C. Steam stripping leverages waste heat and enables easy CO2 re­
covery through combined thermal and concentration driving forces,
while temperature swing offers simpler operation without steam inte­
gration. However, both methods are constrained by the thermal sensi­
tivity of PEI above 135 [◦] C, where degradation can compromise longterm sorbent performance.

_4.5.1._ _Temperature-dependent DAC performance_

Previous sections highlighted the unique properties and limitations

The efficiency of DAC systems is significantly influenced by local
environmental conditions, particularly temperature and humidity.

for optimizing DAC deployment across diverse geographical locations.

_5.1.2._ _Thermal conductivity considerations of materials_

Efficient thermal management is essential for achieving reliable and
durable DAC performance, especially when employing amine-based
solid adsorbents. The thermal conductivity of a sorbent directly im­
pacts its cyclic stability and adsorption–desorption efficiency [127]. The
highly exothermic nature of CO2 chemisorption with amines (typically

_>_ 60 kJ mol [−] [1] ) can result in substantial temperature increases during
operation, which, if not properly managed, can degrade sorbent per­
formance over time. Solid adsorbents supported on low-conductivity
substrates—such as mesoporous silica, clay, ionic liquids, and metal
oxides—are particularly vulnerable to localized heating, with temper­
ature spikes reaching up to ~135 [◦] C during CO2 adsorption [128–130].
These excessive temperature rises can cause thermal degradation of
amines, leading to reduced long-term efficiency and durability of the
sorbent system [131–134]. Therefore, enhancing thermal conductivity
is a crucial aspect of improving DAC performance.

Recent studies have focused on developing thermally conductive
nanocomposites to address these challenges. For example, Wu et al.
developed a 3D interconnected scaffold composed of hydroxylated
graphene (HG) and hydroxylated carbon nanotubes (HCNTs), materials
known for their high intrinsic thermal conductivity [127]. By covalently
grafting PEI onto the HG–HCNT surface, they created a nanocomposite
that maintained structural integrity and adsorption performance even at
desorption temperatures of up to 135 [◦] C. This study demonstrates the
importance of efficient heat dissipation for maintaining sorbent stability
and efficiency.

Parallel research efforts have also explored strategies to improve
thermal transport in MOFs for DAC applications [135–137]. One
approach involves enhancing interfacial thermal conductance (ITC) by
incorporating water adsorbates, which provide additional thermal
pathways between MOFs and their substrates. For instance, Wang et al.
reported a ~7.1-fold increase in ITC at Au/HKUST-1 heterointerfaces
when water molecules are introduced, significantly enhancing heat
transfer and cooling behavior under DAC conditions [135]. Further
investigation into HKUST-1 revealed that its high crystallinity enables
phonon mean free paths of up to 120 nm. However, moderate water
adsorption results in strong phonon scattering, which decreases thermal
conductivity by approximately 51.3 %. Interestingly, additional water
uptake can recover thermal conductivity by forming new thermal
pathways through water bridging within the porous structure [136].
This finding suggests that optimizing water content and interfacial in­
teractions within MOFs can significantly enhance their thermal prop­
erties and, consequently, their DAC performance.

Overall, these findings highlight the importance of tailoring the
thermal transport properties of sorbents to enhance operational stability
and efficiency. Future research should prioritize the development of
advanced materials, such as metal-doped MOFs and nanostructured
composites, that offer high thermal conductivity alongside strong CO2
adsorption capacity. These innovations are critical for enabling durable
and energy-efficient DAC technologies suitable for long-term operation
under realistic conditions.


_5.2._ _Economic considerations of materials_


The cost feasibility analysis highlights key trade-offs in DAC material
selection (Table 9). ACs offer the lowest cost per ton of CO2 captured due
to its widespread availability and relatively simple processing, making it
an attractive option for large-scale deployment. Zeolites also present a



cost-effective alternative, but their sensitivity to moisture limits their
practical application in humid environments. On the higher end, MOFs
have shown exceptional CO2 adsorption capacities but remain costprohibitive due to complex synthesis processes. COFs, while more
scalable than MOFs, still require further advancements to reduce pro­
duction costs. Amine-functionalized silica and alumina strike a balance
between cost, stability, and performance, making them practical choices
for real-world DAC applications, especially in varying humidity condi­
tions. Understanding these cost implications is crucial for determining
the most viable materials for commercial DAC deployment. Future
research should focus on reducing synthesis costs for high-performance
materials while improving the stability and efficiency of lower-cost
alternatives.


_5.3._ _Future research directions_


_5.3.1._ _Challenges and opportunities for DAC advancement_

The advancement of DAC technology requires concentrated research
efforts in the following key areas [139].


A. **Material Development:** The development of robust adsorbents with
enhanced stability under extreme environmental conditions is
crucial, particularly for regions subject to significant temperature
and humidity fluctuations. In parallel, improving regeneration effi­

requires substantial consideration [140]. Additionally, reducing the
costs of support materials and functionalization processes is essential
to make DAC commercially viable [114].
B. **System Integration and Optimization:** A major challenge lies in

systems must involve climate-specific designs that consider local
environmental conditions while maintaining cost-effectiveness.
Enhancing operational flexibility to cope with seasonal variations
and unexpected weather events is also critical. Additionally, the
economic feasibility of DAC systems depends on local energy costs,
infrastructure readiness, and potential revenue from carbon credits

proton transfer mechanisms during CO2 release.

regeneration via catalysis and amine blending can dramatically
improve the energy profile and longevity of DAC systems. While these
innovations have largely been demonstrated in liquid-phase systems,
they provide a valuable framework for developing next-generation solid
sorbents. Adapting such catalytic strategies to the solid phase, in a way
that respects the structural and thermal properties of solid supports, may
enable lower-temperature regeneration, improved sorbent lifetime, and
reduced energy costs in solid sorbent-based DAC technologies.



_5.3.3._ _Integration of DAC and CO2_ _methanation_

An emerging strategy to enhance the efficiency and economic
viability of DAC is the integration of CO2 capture and conversion into a
single system using dual function materials (DFMs) [148–150]. This
approach eliminates intermediate steps such as CO2 compression, pu­
rification, and transport by enabling in situ catalytic conversion of
captured CO2, thereby reducing energy demand and system complexity

[151].

One of the most widely studied conversion pathways is CO2 metha­
nation, where CO2 is hydrogenated into methane (CH4) via the Sabatier
reaction [149]:

CO2 + 4H2 → CH4 + 2H2O, ΔH298K = − 164 kJ mol [−] [1           ] (6)

This exothermic reaction is typically catalyzed by metals such as
nickel (Ni) [152] or ruthenium (Ru) [153], with the latter offering su­
perior activity at lower temperatures and improved resistance to deac­
tivation [152,153]. DFMs typically consist of an alkali-based CO2
sorbent (e.g., Na2O) combined with a dispersed metal catalyst (e.g., Ru)
on a high-surface-area support such as γ-Al2O3. These materials enable
both CO2 adsorption and methanation within a single solid phase,
streamlining the process and improving energy efficiency.

A particularly notable study by Abdallah et al. demonstrated the
long-term stability and effectiveness of a DFM consisting of 1 % Ru and
10 % Na2O/γ-Al2O3 coated onto a ceramic monolith with a high open
frontal area to minimize pressure drop [153]. This material underwent
more than 100 DAC-methanation cycles (exceeding 450 h) under
simulated ambient conditions and maintained stable performance
without observable degradation of either the catalyst or the sorbent.
Importantly, the system showed excellent tolerance to temperature and



humidity fluctuations, continuous representative of real-world DAC
environments. The presence of moisture was even found to enhance CO2
capture, and no performance loss was observed during repeated thermal
cycling—demonstrating the long-term durability of DFMs. Additionally,
the monolithic structure addressed a critical challenge in DAC: mini­
mizing pressure drop in high-throughput air contactors. When paired
with renewable hydrogen, this approach enables the direct production
of synthetic natural gas (CH4) from atmospheric CO2, creating a
closed-loop carbon cycle. These findings underscore the promise of
DFMs in enabling modular, scalable, and energy-efficient DAC systems,
particularly for off-grid or distributed energy applications.


_5.4._ _Recommendations for commercial implementation_


The successful transition from laboratory-scale research to the
commercial deployment of DAC technology hinges on several strategic
considerations. Based on our comprehensive analysis, key recommen­
dations are outlined below to guide future development and commer­
cialization efforts.


A. **Material Cost Reduction:** Reducing production costs of highperformance adsorbents is critical for the commercial scalability of
DAC systems. Efforts should focus on cost-effective manufacturing
techniques for advanced materials, such as hierarchical silica sup­
ports, without compromising their structural integrity or CO2 cap­
ture efficiency. In actual practice, the utilization of MOFs in DAC is
limited due to their limited bulk-scale production and high capital
cost. Despite the vast potential, the high cost associated with the
precursor chemicals and machinery for the production of MOFs in
large quantities retards its viability at present. However, the use of
MOF on polymer support or MOF–polymer nanocomposite could
decrease the overall cost of the adsorbent [138]. The porous polymer
support with low specific heat would be beneficial in transferring
heat away from the material, thereby aiding in desorption [154].
Recently, Tran et al. [155] developed a highly porous membrane
adsorbent composed of branched polyethyleneimine (PEI) impreg­
nated into a low-cost, porous Solupor support (Fig. 8). They also
conducted multiple adsorption-desorption cycles to evaluate its
regenerability, stability, and practical applicability.
Membrane-based DAC is expected to become an important addition
to the portfolio of methods aimed at realizing ubiquitous CO2 cap­
ture, as it holds great potential for unprecedented improvements in
capture cost, installation applications, and environmental impact
compared to the current sorbent-based DAC. Furthermore, it is
believed that this could change the outlook on CO2 capture in the
near future [156].


B. **Operational Longevity:** Ensuring high performance over numerous
adsorption-desorption cycles is crucial for the long-term economic
and operational success of commercial DAC facilities. Future efforts
should prioritize boosting the stability of adsorbents under actual
environmental conditions, addressing resistance to atmospheric
pollutants and variations in temperature and humidity. While amineimpregnated adsorbents are known for their robust CO2 capture
abilities due to high amine levels, they typically encounter issues
with cyclic stability because of amine leaching [25]. Alternatively,
amine-grafted adsorbents, known for their enhanced thermocyclic,
moisture, and oxidative stability, offer a promising solution. How­
ever, it is essential to focus on improving the CO2 adsorption effi­
ciency of these amine-grafted adsorbents [157]. Additionally,
advancements like the HSC [+] supported PSA system developed for
space shuttle applications, which can operate hundreds of cycles and
capture up to 4 % CO2 from dry air at ambient pressure with
regeneration under a 1.33 mbar vacuum, highlight the potential for
further innovation in this field [158]. The domain of DAC using
amine-based adsorbents is still developing, presenting significant

C. **Energy Efficiency:** To enhance sustainability and reduce the energy
requirements of the CO2 desorption process, exploring renewable

amine-based adsorbents that regenerate at lower temperatures
(80–120 [◦] C) can significantly decrease CO2 capture expenses [25].
The energy needed for sorbent regeneration could be sourced from
various cost-effective thermal energy options, including heat pumps,
geothermal sources, and waste heat from industrial activities such as
combined heat and power plants, waste incinerators, and electro­
lyzers, as well as from synthetic fuel production processes [159]. The
use of temperature and vacuum swing desorption (TVSD) in practice,
exemplified by the Climeworks facility in Hinwil, demonstrates how
combined heating and vacuum can effectively regenerate high purity
CO2 at temperatures below 100 [◦] C using waste heat from municipal
waste incineration [160]. Furthermore, moisture swing adsorbents,
such as the amine-based ion exchange resin highlighted by Lackner
et al., offer great potential due to their low-temperature regeneration
capabilities (45 [◦] C) [25].


The commercial success of DAC systems requires collaborative ef­
forts across academia, industry, and government sectors [26,161].
Establishing international collaboration and standardizing performance
metrics will facilitate technological advancements and streamline the
commercialization process. Additionally, implementing supportive
policies and economic incentives can foster investment and innovation,
enabling DAC to become a vital tool for mitigating climate change.

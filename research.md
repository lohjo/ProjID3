# Seven high-quality sources for CO₂ sorbent regeneration parametric study

Below are seven peer-reviewed primary research sources, none overlapping with the five references already in your library, covering all five sub-topics. Sources come predominantly from *Chemical Engineering Journal*, *Industrial & Engineering Chemistry Research*, and *Chemical Engineering Science* — all on your preferred-journals list. One source (Elfving et al. 2021) is listed once but tagged to three sub-topics because it uniquely addresses regeneration temperature, purge flow, and specific energy within a single jacketed packed-bed experiment that closely mirrors the NUS/SUTD apparatus.

## Source 1 — Bos, Kroeze, Sutanto, Brilman (2018)

**Citation.** Bos, M. J.; Kroeze, V.; Sutanto, S.; Brilman, D. W. F. "Evaluating Regeneration Options of Solid Amine Sorbent for CO₂ Removal." *Industrial & Engineering Chemistry Research* **57**(32), 11141–11153 (2018). DOI: 10.1021/acs.iecr.8b00768.

**Sub-topics addressed.** (1) regeneration temperature and (2) purge gas flow rate.

**Verbatim quotes.**
1. Abstract: *"It was found that when using a purge flow the desorption rate is strongly influenced by the equilibrium between the gas and adsorbed phase. With elevated temperature (>80 °C) both the working capacity and the productivity increase significantly."*
2. Introduction (reproducing/extending Serna-Guerrero findings): *"Flow rate of the purge, temperature, and pressure all have a statistically significant influence on the working capacity but … the effect of temperature is dominant with respect to the desorption rate. TSA at 150 °C was found to be the most attractive with the highest CO₂ adsorption loading and the fastest desorption rate."*

Fixed-bed reactor loaded with commercial Lewatit VP OC 1065; N₂ purge flow (e.g., 226 mL/min, Fig. 7) and desorption temperature (40–100+ °C) are varied — a direct analog for the student's parametric sweeps.

## Source 2 — Elfving, Kauppinen, Jegoroff, Ruuskanen, Järvinen, Sainio (2021)

**Citation.** Elfving, J.; Kauppinen, J.; Jegoroff, M.; Ruuskanen, V.; Järvinen, L.; Sainio, T. "Experimental comparison of regeneration methods for CO₂ concentration from air using amine-based adsorbent." *Chemical Engineering Journal* **404**, 126337 (2021). DOI: 10.1016/j.cej.2020.126337. Open access.

**Sub-topics addressed.** (1) regeneration temperature, (2) purge gas flow rate, and (4) energy consumption.

**Verbatim quotes.**
1. Abstract: *"For all other studied regeneration options except TVSA without purge flow, over 85% regeneration was obtained already at 60 °C. Isobaric TSA at 60 °C had the lowest regeneration SER of 4.2 MJ/kgCO₂. Coupling TSA with mild vacuum improved desorption rate and increased working capacity from 0.47 to 0.51 mmolCO₂/g_sorbent, requiring 7.5 MJ/kgCO₂ for regeneration."*
2. Experimental (apparatus description, §2.2): *"The adsorbent sample is placed on the top of a sieve welded into the column and fixed from top by placing quartz wool between the sample and a sieve that is attached to the thermocouple. Temperature of the sample is measured radially from the middle of the sample and axially 10 mm above the lower sieve."*

Jacketed steel packed column, amine resin, compares air/N₂/no purge at 40 and 1000 mL/min and 60 °C/100 °C — the single best methodological template for your project.

## Source 3 — Serna-Guerrero, Belmabkhout, Sayari (2010)

**Citation.** Serna-Guerrero, R.; Belmabkhout, Y.; Sayari, A. "Influence of regeneration conditions on the cyclic performance of amine-grafted mesoporous silica for CO₂ capture: An experimental and statistical study." *Chemical Engineering Science* **65**(14), 4166–4172 (2010). DOI: 10.1016/j.ces.2010.04.029.

**Sub-topics addressed.** (1) regeneration temperature and (2) purge gas flow rate.

**Verbatim quotes.**
1. Abstract: *"Using a 2³ factorial design of experiments, the impact on the performance of the adsorbent of different levels of temperature, pressure, and flow rate of purge gas during desorption was determined."*
2. Abstract: *"It was found that all the parameters under study have a statistically significant influence on the working adsorption capacity, but only temperature is influential with respect to desorption rate."*

Foundational Design-of-Experiments parametric study on triamine-grafted PE-MCM-41. Published in 2010 — outside your 2015–2025 window — but methodologically essential because it is the canonical factorial-design reference isolating temperature, pressure, and purge flow effects.

## Source 4 — Zanco, Ambrosetti, Groppi, Tronconi, Mazzotti (2022)

**Citation.** Zanco, S. E.; Ambrosetti, M.; Groppi, G.; Tronconi, E.; Mazzotti, M. "Heat transfer intensification with packed open-cell foams in TSA processes for CO₂ capture." *Chemical Engineering Journal* **430**, 131000 (2022). DOI: 10.1016/j.cej.2021.131000. Open access.

**Sub-topics addressed.** (5) axial temperature profiles and (3) bed height / sorbent mass.

**Verbatim quotes.**
1. Abstract (p. 1): *"Packed open-cell foams are proposed as an alternative to standard packed bed configuration commonly adopted for the adsorption columns of a temperature swing adsorption process. … The introduction of the packed foam allows for more rapid temperature transitions and substantial reduction of temperature gradients within the adsorption bed."*
2. §2.1 Adsorption column (p. 2): *"K-type thermocouples … are installed longitudinally at three different positions along the radial direction, namely at the center, close to half-radius length, and close to the vessel wall. These three can slide along the axial direction for most of the bed length to obtain a 2D temperature map during steady-state heat transfer experiments…"*

80.2 mm × 800 mm jacketed cylindrical column with 13X pellets; the 2-D (axial + radial) thermocouple layout is a gold-standard template for measurement planning against an electrical-heat-plate wall boundary.

## Source 5 — Marx, Joss, Hefti, Mazzotti (2016)

**Citation.** Marx, D.; Joss, L.; Hefti, M.; Mazzotti, M. "Temperature Swing Adsorption for Postcombustion CO₂ Capture: Single- and Multicolumn Experiments and Simulations." *Industrial & Engineering Chemistry Research* **55**(5), 1401–1412 (2016). DOI: 10.1021/acs.iecr.5b03727.

**Sub-topics addressed.** (5) axial temperature profiles.

**Verbatim quotes.**
1. Abstract: *"A mathematical model used to describe cyclic adsorption processes is calibrated and validated for the simulation of temperature swing adsorption (TSA) processes … During these experiments, the temperature was measured at five positions along the central axis of the column, and the exit composition was measured on line by mass spectrometry."*
2. Abstract: *"The second series were heating and cooling experiments, which were used to study the heat transfer from the heating fluid in the column jacket to the bed. … cyclic TSA experiments were performed to test the model's ability to predict the cyclic steady state behavior of the column as well as the separation performance of the process."*

Provides calibrated bed-side heat-transfer coefficients (SI) and the 1-D non-isothermal model form you will likely reuse in COMSOL or MATLAB simulations.

DOI: 10.1016/j.jtice.2024.105681.

**Sub-topics addressed.** (3) bed height and sorbent mass.

**Verbatim quotes.**
1. Results, §3 / Fig. 2: *"A shorter mass transfer zone was evident with a 10 cm packed bed height, contributing to an earlier breakthrough point and a more abrupt increase in the effluent CO₂ [concentration]."*
2. Abstract: *"The adsorption performance of the adsorbents was assessed using dynamic adsorption capacity, adsorbent bed exhaustion rate (AER), the mass transfer zone (MTZ) at 10 % and 90 % breakthrough levels, and adsorption bed utilization (ABU). … The highest CO₂ binding capacity was 6.12 mg/g at ambient conditions (25 °C and 101.325 kPa)."*

Cite specifically for bed-height → MTZ/breakthrough/ABU/AER; use the Mazzotti-group papers (Sources 4–5) for the heat-transfer aspect of bed geometry.

## Source 7 — Drechsler, Agar (2020)

**Citation.** Drechsler, C.; Agar, D. W. "Concentrated Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) from Diluted Sources through Continuous Temperature Swing Adsorption (TSA)." *Industrial & Engineering Chemistry Research* **59**(20), 9207–9214 (2020). DOI: 10.1021/acs.iecr.9b06177. Open access.

**Sub-topics addressed.** (4) energy consumption.

**Verbatim quotes.**
1. Results, p. 9210: *"For Case 1, the heat of adsorption is in a similar order of magnitude (2.53 MJ/kgCO₂ instead of 1.81–1.83 MJ/kgCO₂), however, the total energy demand is dominated by sensible heat (7.11 MJ/kgCO₂), blower power (8.26 MJ/kgCO₂) and steam production…"*
2. Discussion, p. 9211: *"When steam is used on the desorber side, there is a trade-off between stripping steam demand and increased working capacity. … less solids circulation rate is required and the sensible heat demand in the temperature swing is lower. However, the production of steam comes at a certain cost … a minimum in total heat demand can be identified for any stage configuration."*

Provides the component-wise heat-duty framework (Q_sens = mCₚΔT on sorbent + column; Q_des from ΔH_ads·n_CO₂; parasitic blower/vacuum work) you need to structure your own energy-consumption calculations.

## Synthesis — what the literature says about regeneration parameter sensitivity

Across these sources, **regeneration temperature dominates desorption kinetics**, while purge flow, bed geometry, and sorbent mass primarily shape working capacity, thermal profile uniformity, and specific energy requirement. Serna-Guerrero, Bos, and Elfving converge on the finding that raising temperature from 60 °C to 100–150 °C accelerates desorption and lifts working capacity, but returns diminish once equilibrium-limited regions are reached and cyclic degradation accelerates. Purge gas flow mainly suppresses CO₂ partial pressure in the bulk phase, improving recovery at high flows but penalizing specific energy (Elfving reports 4.2–8.6 MJ/kg CO₂). Bed height and sorbent mass control the mass-transfer-zone length and bed utilization (Hanh), yet excess sorbent inflates sensible-heat duty; Zanco explicitly shows that reducing inventory 26.5% can lift productivity 80% when heat transfer is rate-limiting. Marx and Zanco demonstrate that axial–radial temperature gradients from wall heating govern cycle time and should be designed around rather than neglected.
